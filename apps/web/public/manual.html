I The Quest

Lead a band of adventurers down through a cave of many levels, gather what treasure you can, and climb back to the surface alive.

The cave is built one card at a time as you explore. Behind every doorway may lie an empty tunnel, a chamber heavy with gold, a sleeping dragon, or a trap that drops you into the dark below. Nothing is fixed until you turn it over.

Your score is the worth of everyone who walks back out into the daylight, plus the treasure they carry. A party wiped out underground scores nothing — so the real art is knowing when to press deeper and when to turn for home. This edition is a solitaire game: your progress is saved automatically on this device.

II Building Your Party

Spend up to 6 selection points recruiting your expedition before you enter.

Each creature costs points to recruit and is worth points if it survives the cave. Stronger, more valuable creatures cost more, so a typical party is a careful mix — muscle to fight, backs to carry treasure, and a specialist or two. You may take up to twelve members, but the 6-point budget is the real limit.

A Priest contributes magical power in fights even while standing safely behind the front line. Carrying capacity matters too: an Ogre or a Giant can haul far more treasure home than a Dwarf.

III Exploring the Cave

Move through glowing doorways and stairways; each new step turns over the next area.

Moving

  • Click a glowing doorway marker, or press N / E / S / W, to step that way.
  • Press U / D (or click the corner stair markers) to climb or descend a stairway.
  • Drag to orbit, scroll to zoom. Snap to current tile gives a clean overhead view; the Levels buttons focus a single floor; Free orbit shows the whole cave.

A doorway only leads somewhere if its edge matches the area beyond it. If it doesn't, you've found a dead end — that exit closes, but you may try another. When you descend, the floor you came from is hidden automatically so it never blocks your view of where you now stand.

Drawing a chamber

The first time you enter a chamber you draw small cards onto its floor — and the deeper you are, the more the cave throws at you:

The HUD at a glance

  • Top bar — current depth, turn, party size, and cards left in the deck.
  • Compass & prompt — your orientation and a running description of what just happened.
  • Card panel (right) — the art and name of the area or card under focus.

IV Special Areas

Five named areas behave unlike ordinary tunnels and chambers.

V Hazards

When a chamber turns up a hazard, it strikes immediately — before any treasure or strangers are dealt with.

If several appear at once they resolve in this order: Earthquake → Medusa → Ghouls → Mutiny → Trap.

Earthquake

The area you just came from collapses. It is removed from play — drawn as rubble, impassable, and the doorway back to it is sealed. You cannot return the way you came. The earthquake card is left lying on the rubble to mark what fell.

Medusa

Her gaze sweeps the party; a die is rolled for each member, and a 1 or 2 turns that member to stone (the rolls are shown one per member). A petrified member is marked with a stone badge and can do nothing until freed by the Magic Staff (a wizard bearing the staff is immune). Medusa lurks for good: her card stays on the chamber floor, and she rolls against the party again every time you re-enter. If her gaze petrifies your whole party, the expedition ends there.

Ghouls

Each member fights a round against the ghouls (strength 2) at full fighting strength — a Magic Sword or Strength Potion counts — and anyone who loses is slain (the rolls are shown per member; no surprise on either side). The attack forces the party to drop all heavy treasure onto the floor, which you may reclaim once the fight is over. Like Medusa, ghouls lurk: their card stays in the chamber and they attack again whenever you re-enter. The Talisman wards them off entirely.

Mutiny

Your befriended allies desert — reverting to strangers and dropping any treasure they carried back onto the floor. If your party is made up entirely of allies, one loyal soul remains. You then face the deserters as you would any group of strangers.

Trap

The floor gives way and the party plunges to the area directly below — and there is no climbing back. A Dwarf guides the party safely past one trap (but not two in the same chamber).

No way back A trap fall is one-way. You cannot withdraw or retreat back up, so plan to find another route out — or a secret stairway down and around.

VI Strangers & Reactions

When a chamber holds creatures, you must decide how to meet them before touching any treasure there.

You have three choices, taken in the same turn:

  • Withdraw — back out the way you came, leaving the strangers and any treasure behind.
  • Attack — fight at once (see Fights). Striking the instant you enter a fresh chamber gains you the advantage of surprise.
  • Test their reaction — approach and roll to see how they feel about you.

The leader, and the reaction roll

One stranger leads the group, by this order of priority:

Spectre › Dragon › Wizard › Hero / Woman Hero › Priest › Man / Woman › Giant › Ogre › Troll › Dwarf

Roll a die for the leader; the rest react the same way. Charisma adds +1 and each curse subtracts 1 — but a natural 1 is always hostile, no matter the bonuses. The result is one of:

  • Friendly — they join your party as allies, and the chamber's treasure is yours.
  • Indifferent — they ignore you for now. Next turn you may test again, attack, or leave. Test to indifferent three times and they become permanently indifferent to your party: you may then walk on out by any exit, though the treasure stays guarded (you can't take it), and they stay indifferent if you return.
  • Hostile — they attack at once, and a round is fought (with the strangers holding the advantage of surprise).
Who reacts how Gentle folk are easily befriended; a Dragon or Spectre almost never is. Bringing a charismatic leader, and avoiding curses, swings the odds your way.

VII Fights

A fight runs one round per turn until one side is wiped out, put to sleep, or your party retreats.

Surprise

The side with surprise adds +1 to all its rolls in the first round. Your party gains it by attacking immediately on entering a chamber by a fresh doorway (or by magic carpet). You do not get it if you stopped to test the strangers first, or if you fell in through a trap. Strangers gain surprise when they turn hostile on being approached.

Pairing off

Each fighter is matched against a stranger and a die is rolled for each side; total strength plus the die decides the winner, and the loser is slain. A tie kills no one.

  • If you outnumber the strangers, two of your members may gang up on one foe.
  • If they outnumber you, a single member can be set against at most two foes at once; any further strangers stand idle that round, and you face the strongest combination they can form.
  • Priests and wizards may fight hand-to-hand or hang back, adding their magical power to a front-line fighter. Any number of casters can pool their power against one enemy.

Choosing who falls

When two of your members lose a fight together, you decide which one is lost. State your preference and roll: on a 4, 5 or 6 you get your wish; otherwise the other falls. (The Ring protects its bearer — see below.)

Retreat

After at least one round has been fought, you may retreat by any of the tile's doorways or stairways — even one you haven't explored yet (a tile is drawn for it). The strangers and any treasure dropped in the fight are left behind. But if that way turns out to be a dead end, the party can't escape it and must fight another round this turn — so try a different exit. You cannot retreat before the first round, nor back up a trap you fell through.

They remember Strangers you retreat from stay hostile to your party for the rest of the game — return to that chamber and they attack on sight, with no chance to test them. (Other parties may still approach them.)

Two foes to respect

The Dragon (fighting strength 6) is the deadliest common foe — but slay one single-handed and that hero's worth is doubled at scoring forever. A Charmed Flute lulls dragons to sleep so the party can walk straight past them.

The Spectre cannot be harmed by ordinary blows. Without magic or the Magic Sword in hand, it slays your strongest member each round. The Eye of God destroys spectres outright.

VIII The Sorcerer

The single most powerful dweller in the cave — best avoided, unless you come prepared.

A lone Sorcerer lurks somewhere in the deck. With fighting strength 4 and magical power 9, he fights at an effective strength of 13 — far beyond a Dragon — and as the highest-ranking creature he always leads any group he joins and turns it hostile. He is never befriended, and carries no point value of his own.

He is too mighty to be put to sleep or to have his magic wholly unmade: Lotus Dust and the Eye of God each reduce his Strength by only 2. Bring both and his 13 drops to 9 — still formidable, but no longer hopeless for a strong party. Without them, meeting the Sorcerer is very nearly a death sentence; withdraw if you still can.

Lore In the original boxed game the Sorcerer rules a great cavern on the fourth level — the Sorcerer's Den — and slaying him is worth a 30-point bounty. That competitive scenario is not part of this solitaire edition; here the Sorcerer is simply the deadliest creature you may chance upon.

IX Treasure & Artefacts

Unguarded treasure is yours to pick up; guarded treasure waits until the strangers are befriended or beaten.

Heavy treasure

Silver, Gold and Gems are heavy — they count against a member's carrying capacity. When you pick a piece up, only the members who have the spare capacity to bear it are offered (the party panel lets you move items between members or drop them on the floor). The Treasure Chest is heaviest of all at 100 kg, so only a strong back — a Giant, or an empty-handed Ogre — can carry it. A member must be carrying the chest to open it; do so and a die decides your fortune: a curse, a Spectre, worthless sand, or Silver, Gold, or Gems.

Artefacts weigh nothing and grant powers. Some are used up; others serve as long as you hold them.

X Secret Doors

More often than not, a stairway leads to an area where no matching stair is drawn. That hidden end is a secret door.

When you descend onto a chamber that shows no stair up — or climb onto one with no stair down — your party learns of a secret door there, and can use it to retrace their steps. Each one is marked on the floor with a lettered door card (A, B, C…), laid in the order you discover them, so you can read at a glance how the levels of the cave connect.

The glowing up- or down-stair marker still appears on that tile — step onto it to travel back through the secret door.

Secret door “A”.

XI Leaving the Cave & Scoring

Climb any stairway up from level 1 to leave the cave — and bank your score.

Leaving is one-way: once your party steps out, the expedition is over. You'll be shown a roll call of who made it out and what each carried, with the total tallied. Enter a name to record it on the shared high-score table. You can also end a run at any time with Quit — but quitting abandons your party to the cave: they do not climb out. The score they had gathered is still tallied (a wiped party, of course, scores nothing).

How the score is reckoned

  • The points value of every member who walks out alive (stone and fallen members count for nothing).
  • Plus the points value of all treasure they carry.
  • Dragon-slayers who killed a dragon single-handed count double.
  • Plus any loot banked from the Treasure Chest, and +30 for slaying the Sorcerer.
  • −30 for each curse upon the party.
All or nothing A party wiped out underground scores zero. Greed is the surest way to lose everything — know when to turn for the surface.

XII Multiplayer Games

Up to four parties race the same cave at once, taking turns from their own browsers — first to the richest haul out wins.

Creating and joining a game

  • Create a game from the title screen: choose your party name and a colour, and you become the host. The game is given a unique four-letter code.
  • Share that code with your friends. Each of them picks Join Multiplayer Game, enters the code in their own browser, and chooses a party name and an unused colour (colours already taken are greyed out).
  • Up to four players may gather in the lobby. When everyone's ready the host starts the game — after that no one new can join.

Choosing parties

Everyone drafts their party from one shared pack of creatures, so the draft is taken in turns — a card another player takes is gone for the rest of you. Once the last player has chosen, the expedition begins.

Turn by turn

  • Players take turns in a set order. The “Now” marker in the top bar (in that party's colour) always shows whose turn it is; you can only act on your turn.
  • A turn plays out to a finish — an entire fight, a reaction, picking up treasure — before play passes on, so combat never spills across other players' turns.
  • The cave is shared: it grows as everyone explores, and you'll see the other parties as small coloured tokens on the map where they currently stand.

Keeping in touch

  • Toast messages slide in under the depth bar to announce what every other party does — defeating or befriending creatures, taking treasure, descending or climbing, and finishing their run.
  • A docked chat (bottom-left) lets the players talk; a red dot on the “Chat” tab marks unread messages, and incoming chat is also mirrored as a toast.

When a party finishes

A party's run ends when it escapes the cave, is wiped out, or the player abandons it (the multiplayer Quit → Abandon the expedition). The outcome and score are recorded to a separate multiplayer high-score table and announced to everyone still below. You are not kicked out — you land on the standings, and the whole game ends once every party has finished.

The standings page

A live scoreboard ranks every party by score, showing finished parties' outcomes and the live depth, turns, enemies slain and running score of those still exploring. From it you can:

  • Dip into the cave — drop into a free-roaming, read-only view to watch the action.
  • Follow a party — click any row to see exactly what that player sees, until you go back to the standings.
  • My run — review your own party's final score breakdown (then “Back to Standings”).
  • Leave to menu — your party stays in the game; you can rejoin later with the code. (Abandon, by contrast, ends your run for good.)
Note This first multiplayer release runs the beginner ruleset: parties race the same cave but don't fight, trade with, or block one another — the whole map is open to all.

A few hard-won tips

  • Bring a strong back (Ogre, Troll) — treasure left behind scores nothing.
  • A Dwarf is cheap insurance against a trap; charisma makes strangers into allies.
  • Attack the instant you enter for the surprise bonus — testing first throws it away.
  • Befriending strangers is often safer (and richer) than fighting them.
  • Watch your depth: the deeper you go, the more the cave draws against you.
  • Turn back while you can still carry your winnings into the light.
